1 Evaluation of Pongakawa Playcentre

How well placed is Pongakawa Playcentre to promote positive learning outcomes for children?

Not well placed

Requires further development

Well placed

Very well placed

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Pongakawa Playcentre is a long-established, parent-led early childhood service. It is situated south of Te Puke in the district of Pongakawa and adjacent to the local school. The centre buildings and grounds are spacious, attractive and well maintained. There are mature trees and an orchard for children to explore.

The centre is licensed to cater for 25 children including up to 13 children under the age of two years at any one time. At the time of this ERO review there were 22 children on the roll including 15 under two years and six children identified as being of Māori descent. The centre operates three mixed-age, group-supervised morning sessions each week.

Pongakawa Playcentre operates under the umbrella of the Western Bay of Plenty Playcentre Association (WBOPPA). Centre members benefit from the support and guidance of an experienced centre liaison officer. The centre administration officer provided by the association also works on one session per week to meet qualification requirements. She provides valuable modelling and support for parents. WBOPPA also provide training workshops and useful documentation and frameworks to guide centre operations. These guidelines are underpinned by the association’s philosophy, which is highly evident in members’ practice.

Centre members are engaging in different levels of training and are committed to learning alongside their children. They place priority on promoting children’s learning through play as happy, confident learners who can try new things in a safe and secure environment.

Since the 2011 ERO review centre members have worked effectively with the support of the association and federation to strengthen self review. Members have developed manageable systems and frameworks for planning and assessing children’s learning.

This review was part of a cluster of six playcentre reviews in the Western Bay of Plenty Association.

The Review Findings

Children demonstrate confidence and a strong sense of ownership as they play and learn alongside their friends. They have established trusting relationships with other adults. The mixed-age setting promotes opportunities for older children to be leaders and build their social skills as they interact with younger children and their siblings. Older children are sensitive and careful of infants and toddlers in the learning environment. They explore and make choices from a wide variety of interesting and open-ended equipment. Children have fun taking calculated risks and building their physical skills on appropriately challenging equipment. They express themselves creatively through art, music, writing and imaginative play.

Centre members prepare the environment well and provide interesting experiences that promote positive learning outcomes for children. Routines are flexible and responsive to the diverse needs and abilities of children and their families. Transitions are well planned and contribute to a strong sense of belonging and wellbeing amongst adults and children. The centre maintains close and reciprocal relationships with the local school.

Members enthusiastically share their knowledge of literacy, mathematical and scientific concepts as they work alongside children. Their diverse range of knowledge and experience enriches the programme. However, because of the differing levels of understanding parents have of mathematical learning, there would be benefit in members taking a more intentional approach to reviewing this area of the curriculum. Members provide continuity for learning between home and the centre through their ongoing involvement with the programme. They support children to revisit and share their learning through conversations, displays, children’s individual portfolios and by making books about centre events.

Māori children have good opportunities to experience success as Māori as they learn with and alongside their whānau. Te reo Māori is increasingly used by centre members. Members benefit from another member sharing her knowledge and capability in the use of tikanga and te reo Māori. Playcentre whānau recently prepared and shared a hangi with the wider community as part of Matariki celebrations. Children and their families have participated in kapahaka celebrations at the local school. They are increasing their understanding of New Zealand as a bicultural society.

Members build their understanding of centre operations and develop leadership skills as they participate in ongoing training and take responsibility for key roles to ensure smooth running of centre operations. This shared approach has established strong partnerships with families in the local and wider community. Centre leaders have implemented sound systems of collaborative self review and centre-wide planning, which contribute to ongoing centre sustainability, development and improvement.

Key Next Steps

ERO and centre members agree that the next steps for Pongakawa Playcentre are to:

continue to strengthen programme planning by intentionally responding to children’s emerging learning and interests through the termly and end-of-session evaluations. Particular attention should be given to adding challenge and complexity to older children’s learning.

make greater use of guidelines, frameworks and indicators of best practice to inform self review

consider making greater use of Te Whariki, the early childhood curriculum, to support members to more effectively document learning for infants, toddlers and young children.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Pongakawa Playcentre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service’s systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

Next ERO Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Pongakawa Playcentre will be in three years.

3. General Information about Early Childhood Reviews

ERO’s Evaluation Framework

ERO’s overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‘How well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?’ ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework Ngā Pou Here:

  • Pou Whakahaere – how the service determines its vision, philosophy and direction to ensure positive outcomes for children
  • Pou Ārahi – how leadership is enacted to enhance positive outcomes for children
  • Mātauranga – whose knowledge is valued and how the curriculum is designed to achieve positive outcomes for children
  • Tikanga whakaako – how approaches to teaching and learning respond to diversity and support positive outcomes for children.

Within these areas ERO considers the effectiveness of arotake – self review and of whanaungatanga – partnerships with parents and whānau.

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are Māori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

For more information about the framework and Ngā Pou Here refer to ERO’s Approach to Review in Early Childhood Services.

ERO’s Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Very well placed – The next ERO review in four years
  • Well placed – The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development – The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO’s website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service’s context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
