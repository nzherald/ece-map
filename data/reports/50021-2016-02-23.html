1 Evaluation of Funhouse Learning Centre

How well placed is Funhouse Learning Centre to promote positive learning outcomes for children?

Not well placed

Requires further development

Well placed

Very well placed

Funhouse Learning Centre has recently experienced significant change and high staff turnover. The newly-appointed centre manager and team leaders are systematically improving systems and processes to promote the sustained quality of its provision for children. These developments are at an early stage of implementation.

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Funhouse Learning Centre is one of two privately-owned early childhood education and care facilities. It is licensed to cater for up to 30 children aged over two years. The other Funhouse service, which is adjacent, is for children under two years.

The centre’s directors oversee financial and property-related aspects of its operations. Management of the day-to-day education and care is the responsibility of the centre manager and a team leader. Their priority goal is to support a positive learning environment for children through collaborative, respectful relationships between teachers, parents and whānau.

The philosophy is underpinned by the founder’s mission to provide ‘your child’s home away from home’.

The Review Findings

Children play and learn in an environment that is well resourced and thoughtfully laid out. They freely access a wide range of age-appropriate equipment and materials for exploration, challenge, construction, creativity and dramatic play.

Children’s relationships with each other and with teachers are warm and trusting, with a strong sense of family. Teachers show a caring approach toward all children. They are familiar with and respond to individual needs and strengths.

Teachers are reviewing the ways they assess children’s learning, plan suitable programmes and reflect on their effectiveness in fostering learning and wellbeing. Improvement in these processes is well supported by close collegiality among staff and clearly articulated guidelines and expectations. To better reflect the centre philosophy, leaders recognise that strengthening and fostering child-led learning is a priority. To bring this about, making close, explicit links between assessment, planning and evaluation is the next step.

The centre manager and team leader are developing a robust framework to support continuous improvement in teaching and learning. These include observing practice, giving feedback and documenting evidence of effective teaching in terms of its impact on children’s learning and engagement. New appraisal processes are to be implemented in 2016. These are seen as a key element in promoting consistent quality and enhancing future development.

Provisionally registered teachers receive appropriate mentoring from externally-based early childhood practitioners.

Changes in leadership provide a sound platform from which to develop the organisational conditions for ongoing improvement. Emerging leaders are well supported to build their capability. For the sustained growth of the centre, it is timely to review leadership roles and responsibilities, particularly in relation to administration.

Leaders are highly reflective. Using appropriate evidence, they conduct formal reviews of programmes and operations to improve outcomes for children. It is timely to shift review practice from looking at what they do to evaluating how well teaching and learning practices support improved outcomes. As a starting point staff should develop and document what success and effectiveness look like. This will contribute to a set of clear criteria against which staff can measure the centre’s performance.

Daily routines and programmes reflect the centre’s bicultural and multicultural community. Children hear te reo Māori spoken by staff, and recognise and use Māori words and phrases. Further building teachers’ cultural awareness, knowledge and responsiveness is identified as an important next step in the ongoing integration of tikanga Māori in teaching and learning.

Formal and informal feedback from parents and whānau affirms the friendly, comfortable way in which they are welcomed and included in daily programmes. Their aspirations, ideas and wishes are sought and responded to. Leaders continue to explore ways to increase parent and whānau engagement in the life of the centre.

Key Next Steps

To ensure the centre’s further growth and development, the centre manager and team leaders recognise that they must continue to implement and embed the new systems and processes. ERO’s evaluation supports this view.

Key next steps for leaders are to:

  • reinforce the alignment between assessment, planning and evaluation
  • sharpen internal evaluation practice
  • increase the focus on children’s ownership and direction of their learning
  • ensure that staff are formally appraised
  • review leadership roles, structures and responsibilities.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Funhouse Learning Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service’s systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

Actions for compliance

To meet requirements, the service needs to improve its performance in the following area:

  • fully implement a system of regular appraisal.[Licensing Criteria for Early Childhood Education and Care Centres 2008 GMA7]

Development Plan Recommendation

ERO recommends that the service consult with the Ministry of Education and plan to address the key next steps and actions outlined in this report.

Next ERO Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Funhouse Learning Centre will be within two years.

The Purpose of ERO Reports

The Education Review Office (ERO) is the government department that, as part of its work, reviews early childhood services throughout Aotearoa New Zealand. ERO’s reports provide information for parents and communities about each service’s strengths and next steps for development. ERO’s bicultural evaluation framework Ngā Pou Here is described in SECTION 3 of this report. Early childhood services are partners in the review process and are expected to make use of the review findings to enhance children's wellbeing and learning.

3 General Information about Early Childhood Reviews

ERO’s Evaluation Framework

ERO’s overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‘How well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?’ ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework Ngā Pou Here:

  • Pou Whakahaere – how the service determines its vision, philosophy and direction to ensure positive outcomes for children
  • Pou Ārahi – how leadership is enacted to enhance positive outcomes for children
  • Mātauranga – whose knowledge is valued and how the curriculum is designed to achieve positive outcomes for children
  • Tikanga whakaako – how approaches to teaching and learning respond to diversity and support positive outcomes for children.

Within these areas ERO considers the effectiveness of arotake – self review and of whanaungatanga – partnerships with parents and whānau.

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are Māori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

For more information about the framework and Ngā Pou Here refer to ERO’s Approach to Review in Early Childhood Services.

ERO’s Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Very well placed – The next ERO review in four years
  • Well placed – The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development – The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO’s website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service’s context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
