1 Evaluation of Little Bears Early Childhood Education & Care Centre

How well placed is Little Bears Early Childhood Education & Care Centre to promote positive learning outcomes for children?

Not well placed

Requires further development

Well placed

Very well placed

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Little Bears Early Childhood Education & Care Centre was established in 2010. It provides care and education for mainly Māori, Kiribati and other Pacific Island children. The centre is located in a renovated building in Manurewa. The centre owners have second centre, Big Bears Education and Care, in Wiri, South Auckland.

While Little Bears does not have a history of positive ERO reports, recent changes have resulted in the centre being better placed now to promote positive outcomes for children.

There have been significant changes in centre leadership and management. The internal appointment of a centre manager and programme manager have strengthened the teaching team. All the teachers are qualified. The newly appointed business manager is experienced and knowledgeable. She oversees the operational and administrative aspects of the centre. An office administrator provides support for centre operations.

The centre’s clear philosophy and vision promote children’s learning and are highly evident in teaching practices. The learning programme for children is now child-focused and child-led.Te Whāriki, the early childhood curriculum, is well embedded in the centre’s programme. Teachers’ professional development and self review have resulted in good quality outcomes for children and their families.

ERO is satisfied that the areas for further review and development identified in the 2013 ERO report have been addressed adequately. ERO commends the improvements that the leadership and teaching team have achieved in a short time.

The Review Findings

The curriculum is well designed to promote positive outcomes for children. It is built on children’s strengths and interests and has links to current cultural events and community celebrations. Strong relationships with children and their whānau are a key feature of the centre. As a result, children have a sense of belonging in the centre and settle quickly into the programme.

Children are active, happy, positive learners. They are confident communicators and are respected to initiate their own learning. Children have a sense of ownership in their place of learning and trust adults to be responsive to their needs and interests.

The programme and learning environment are culturally rich and celebrate every child’s unique background. The physical environment is inviting and calm and supports children to learn together and at their own pace. The resources encourage literacy and numeracy learning, are related to children’s interests, and provide challenges in their learning.

The babies and toddlers have large, separate indoor and outdoor spaces to explore safely. Easy access between the indoor and outdoor areas encourages children’s learning and development.

Children’s assessment records demonstrate teachers’ knowledge of learning and of children as learners. Teachers acknowledge and respect the place of Māori in Aotearoa and are committed to a strong bicultural programme. The affirmation of Te Tiriti o Waitangi is an integral part of the centre’s culture. Māori and Pacific children, and children from diverse ethnicities, experience a programme where their identity, culture and language are an integral part of their learning and development.

Another strength of the centre is the cultural diversity of the staff and their multi-lingual skills. The staff work well together and show a strong commitment to improving their teaching practice and to supporting each other to learn each others' languages and culture.

A noteworthy feature of the centre is the authentic partnership between the centre, parents and whānau. Relationships are respectful and responsive to parents’ aspirations for their children.

Parents are active participants and contribute to the programme and to their children’s learning. They appreciate the support of managers and teachers. Parents who spoke to ERO expressed their gratitude for the transportation provided for children to and from the centre. This has increased the attendance of children and improved their participation in early childhood education.

Centre managers are improvement-focused. They work well together with teachers to promote better learning outcomes for children. Effective professional development and self review have impacted positively on programme quality and have resulted in improvements to centre operations and processes. A strategic decision to share the role of centre manager has given opportunities for teachers to rise to the challenge and responsibility of leadership.

Key Next Steps

ERO, centre managers and teachers agree that for the centre to sustain good practice they could:

  • involve parents more in centre operations by consulting with them about the strategic plan and self review
  • develop a set of indicators to show how the centre’s philosophy is implemented
  • include a variety of teachers’ contributions to children’s assessments
  • deepen their analysis of learning and extend opportunities for complex learning through stimulating resources and being more spontaneous in their responses to children about their activities.

Managers should also ensure that non-teaching staff are involved in an appraisal process to improve their practice. The owners could consider accessing an external early childhood advisor to appraise and mentor the administration manager in her centre operations role.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Little Bears Early Childhood Education & Care Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service’s systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

The Purpose of ERO Reports

The Education Review Office (ERO) is the government department that, as part of its work, reviews early childhood services throughout Aotearoa New Zealand. ERO’s reports provide information for parents and communities about each service’s strengths and next steps for development. ERO’s bicultural evaluation framework Ngā Pou Here is described in SECTION 3 of this report. Early childhood services are partners in the review process and are expected to make use of the review findings to enhance children's wellbeing and learning.

3 General Information about Early Childhood Reviews

ERO’s Evaluation Framework

ERO’s overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‘How well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?’ ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework Ngā Pou Here:

  • Pou Whakahaere – how the service determines its vision, philosophy and direction to ensure positive outcomes for children
  • Pou Ārahi – how leadership is enacted to enhance positive outcomes for children
  • Mātauranga – whose knowledge is valued and how the curriculum is designed to achieve positive outcomes for children
  • Tikanga whakaako – how approaches to teaching and learning respond to diversity and support positive outcomes for children.

Within these areas ERO considers the effectiveness of arotake – self review and of whanaungatanga – partnerships with parents and whānau.

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are Māori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

For more information about the framework and Ngā Pou Here refer to ERO’s Approach to Review in Early Childhood Services.

ERO’s Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Very well placed – The next ERO review in four years
  • Well placed – The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development – The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO’s website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service’s context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
