1 Evaluation of Wanaka Preschool Early Childhood Centre

How well placed is Wanaka Preschool Early Childhood Centre to promote positive learning outcomes for children?

Not well placed

Requires further development

Well placed

Very well placed

ERO's findings that support this overall judgement are summarised below.

Background

Wanaka Preschool Early Childhood Centre is a non-profit organisation governed by a parent committee. Children play and learn in two rooms with 1 to 3 ½ year olds in one room and the 3 ½ to 5 year olds in the other. As a result of reducing roll numbers the centre, in consultation with the community, has extended its opening hours and age of entry. The centre has stable staffing with some teachers having been there for more than ten years.

The centre has a strong emphasis on environmental sustainability and fosters, through its programmes, children’s engagement with and respect for the environment. Parent involvement in their children’s learning is highly valued and the success of some daily programmes depends on them taking part.

Children of various ethnicities and those with particular health needs are well included in the life of the centre.

The recommendation from the 2012 ERO review for the centre to use self review to make improvements has led to positive changes. This review has identified that further improvements could be made to the recording of self-review findings.

The Review Findings

Children take part in a wide variety of experiences within and beyond the centre. These experiences include learning early literacy and numeracy skills. Teachers plan the daily programmes around children’s interests, parents’ aspirations, promoting the local environment and environ-education for sustainability.

Children learn and play in spacious areas. Their learning is supported by easily accessed, high-quality equipment and resources. The physical environment has been adapted to enable children under two to easily access resources and manage their own play.

Teachers strive to base their practices on current research. Routines are now more relaxed to minimise interruptions to children’s learning and play. Children demonstrate a strong sense of belonging in the centre. Older children were observed playing together for sustained periods of time.

There are strong relationships between teachers, parents and children and interactions are respectful. Teachers use a wide range of ways to communicate with parents about the aspirations they have for their children’s learning and to deepen their knowledge of the children in their care. Teachers are very responsive to the parents’ requests for their children and plan well to support the learning needs of the children.

Assessment practices identify children’s learning effectively and provide useful information for daily programmes. Teachers’ planning for individual children could show more specifically the strategies they plan to use to support children’s learning. More detailed planning will provide a better basis for teachers to evaluate the impact of their teaching.

Teachers are proactive in providing an environment where Māori children and their whānau can see and hear their culture reflected within the centre. This also allows all children to learn and play within a bicultural setting.

The centre has clearly set out its purposes and developed useful strategic objectives. These objectives are well aligned to the vision, values and philosophy. The annual plan provides the detail of how strategic goals will be implemented within the current year.

The staff and governing committee have close links with the parent community and regularly gather information from them so that they can best serve the community and children. Decisions made at the governance level are well informed by self-review findings. Concern for children’s wellbeing and learning are priorities for the teachers and committee and there are effective systems to monitor the physical and emotional safety for all involved in the centre.

The senior teacher has developed useful practices, systems and guidelines for supporting teachers’ planning. Close communication amongst teachers means that the expectations for teaching and children’s learning are closely followed. The senior leaders place a priority on ongoing improvement and provide well for teachers’ professional learning and development (PLD). The appraisal process is useful and leads to PLD based on extending teachers’ expertise and providing for children’s learning and wellbeing requirements. Authentic opportunities for teachers to build leadership skills are provided.

The Preschool has positive relationships with local primary schools and other early childhood centres. This helps support children as they move on from the centre. The senior teacher regularly reports to the committee about any issues and what is happening for children at the centre. She acknowledges that these reports could be more evaluative. More evaluative information would show what difference teachers are making to children’s learning.

Key Next Steps

The senior leaders agree that:

  • further improvements could be made to their self-review processes
  • teachers could better record the strategies they plan to use to extend children’s learning
  • reports to the committee could better show the difference teachers are making to children’s learning.

Management Assurance on Legal Requirements

Before the review, the staff and management of Wanaka Preschool Early Childhood Centre completed an ERO Centre Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist. In these documents they attested that they have taken all reasonable steps to meet their legal obligations related to:

  • curriculum
  • premises and facilities
  • health and safety practices
  • governance, management and administration.

During the review, ERO looked at the service’s systems for managing the following areas that have a potentially high impact on children's wellbeing:

  • emotional safety (including positive guidance and child protection)
  • physical safety (including supervision; sleep procedures; accidents; medication; hygiene; excursion policies and procedures)
  • suitable staffing (including qualification levels; police vetting; teacher registration; ratios)
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

All early childhood services are required to promote children's health and safety and to regularly review their compliance with legal requirements.

Next ERO Review

When is ERO likely to review the service again?

The next ERO review of Wanaka Preschool Early Childhood Centre will be in three years.

The Purpose of ERO Reports

The Education Review Office (ERO) is the government department that, as part of its work, reviews early childhood services throughout Aotearoa New Zealand. ERO’s reports provide information for parents and communities about each service’s strengths and next steps for development. ERO’s bicultural evaluation framework Ngā Pou Here is described in SECTION 3 of this report. Early childhood services are partners in the review process and are expected to make use of the review findings to enhance children's wellbeing and learning.

3 General Information about Early Childhood Reviews

ERO’s Evaluation Framework

ERO’s overarching question for an early childhood education review is ‘How well placed is this service to promote positive learning outcomes for children?’ ERO focuses on the following factors as described in the bicultural framework Ngā Pou Here:

  • Pou Whakahaere – how the service determines its vision, philosophy and direction to ensure positive outcomes for children
  • Pou Ārahi – how leadership is enacted to enhance positive outcomes for children
  • Mātauranga – whose knowledge is valued and how the curriculum is designed to achieve positive outcomes for children
  • Tikanga whakaako – how approaches to teaching and learning respond to diversity and support positive outcomes for children.

Within these areas ERO considers the effectiveness of arotake – self review and of whanaungatanga – partnerships with parents and whānau.

ERO evaluates how well placed a service is to sustain good practice and make ongoing improvements for the benefit of all children at the service.

A focus for the government is that all children, especially priority learners, have an opportunity to benefit from quality early childhood education. ERO will report on how well each service promotes positive outcomes for all children, with a focus on children who are Māori, Pacific, have diverse needs, and are up to the age of two.

For more information about the framework and Ngā Pou Here refer to ERO’s Approach to Review in Early Childhood Services.

ERO’s Overall Judgement and Next Review

The overall judgement that ERO makes and the timing of the next review will depend on how well placed a service is to promote positive learning outcomes for children. The categories are:

  • Very well placed – The next ERO review in four years
  • Well placed – The next ERO review in three years
  • Requires further development – The next ERO review within two years
  • Not well placed - The next ERO review in consultation with the Ministry of Education

ERO has developed criteria for each category. These are available on ERO’s website.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews are tailored to each service’s context and performance, within the overarching review framework. The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to the service.
