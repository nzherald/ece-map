2 The Education Review Office (ERO) Evaluation

The Sandbox Preschool is a privately owned, all day education and care service catering for children from birth to school age.  It is located in central Papamoa and is licensed for 40 children including a maximum of eight under two years of age.  This is the first ERO review of the service.  The centre philosophy promotes a partnership between parents, whānau and teachers working together to benefit children’s learning and development.

The centre opened in 2007 and since that time the owners have created an exceptional learning environment for infants, toddlers and young children.  In addition, they and the management team have developed a focused approach to self review and strategic planning, which is providing a sound framework for ongoing improvement and a clear centre direction.

Teachers are highly focused on promoting and enhancing each child’s emotional and physical wellbeing.  Children are safe, happy and well cared for within a home-like setting.  Teachers gather a range of information about children’s interests and learning which they share with parents, whom they encourage to contribute ideas about their child’s development and interests.

Children benefit from skilled teaching interactions where their ideas and interests are extended as they develop as capable and confident learners.  Each child is supported to develop interests in reading, writing and mathematics through meaningful learning opportunities. 

Teachers also promote children’s social development through modelling and teaching ways to solve conflicts, negotiate and how to be a good friend.  These experiences are likely to provide children with the confidence and skills needed to experience success in their learning now and in the future.

Teachers give priority to respectful inclusion of the Māori language and culture to reflect New Zealand’s bicultural heritage in the centre.  Children enjoy this aspect of centre life.  Children’s play and exploration are valued by teachers as meaningful learning and as a result children are becoming increasingly independent learners and decision makers. 

Future Action

ERO is confident that the service is being managed in the interests of the children.  Therefore ERO is likely to review the service again as part of the regular review cycle.

3 Review Priorities

The Focus of the Review

Before the review, the management of The Sandbox Preschool was invited to consider its priorities for review using guidelines and resources provided by ERO.  ERO also used documentation provided by the centre to contribute to the scope of the review.

The detailed priorities for review were then determined following a discussion between the ERO review team and the management and staff.  This discussion focused on existing information held by the centre (including self-review information) and the extent to which potential issues for review contributed to positive outcomes for children atThe Sandbox Preschool.

All ERO education reviews in early childhood focus on the quality of education.  For ERO this includes the quality of:

  • the programme provided for children;
  • the learning environment; and
  • the interactions between children and adults.

ERO’s findings in these areas are set out below.

The Quality of Education

Background

The Sandbox Preschool was established in 2007 to cater for children and families in Papamoa, which is one of the fastest growing areas in the Bay of Plenty.  The centre philosophy promotes a partnership between parents, whānau and teachers working together to benefit children’s learning and development.  The centre aims to provide dynamic and creative learning opportunities where children are willing to take risks in a safe, supportive environment and view themselves as capable and confident learners.  ERO’s review considers the influence of this philosophy on the quality of education provided for children and families. 

Areas of good performance

The philosophy in action:  The Sandbox Preschool’s philosophy is strongly reflected in the partnership with parents, the programme and the learning environment.  There are significant opportunities for children to learn through meaningful, complex and real life experiences.  Teachers encourage children’s innovation and creativity.  They trust children as capable, competent learners who willingly set their own challenges and take risks within a safe and well-monitored environment.  The philosophy is well understood by teachers and clearly conveyed to parents.  The centre philosophy in action is contributing to the provision of a high quality service for infants, toddlers and young children.

The Māori dimension:  The acknowledgement of the unique place of Māori as tangata whenua is a priority for centre management and teachers.  The core values of whanaungatanga, kotahitanga and manaakitanga underpin all aspects of centre practice.  Teachers show genuine respect for Māori culture through the inclusion of te reo and tikanga Māori in meaningful contexts throughout each day.  Children enjoy this aspect of centre life and as a result they are developing a growing appreciation and understanding of Māori language and protocols.

Child-initiated play:  Children’s play and exploration are valued by teachers as meaningful learning.  The programme offers extensive opportunities for uninterrupted, self-initiated play, where children can make decisions and develop complex play scenarios.  In addition, children have the time and space to explore the environment, choose resources and develop genuine friendships through creative play.  Children are becoming increasingly independent learners and decision makers.

Teaching and learning interactions:  Teachers are skilled at identifying children’s strengths and interests and extending their ideas and thinking.  Specific strategies used by teachers include open-ended questioning, active listening and purposefully engaging children in meaningful conversations.  Teachers challenge children to research, ask questions and develop their own theories about how things work in the physical, social and natural world.  These skilled interactions are positively influencing children’s view of themselves as capable and confident learners.

The learning environment:  The centre owners have created an exceptional learning environment that motivates the curiosity of infants, toddlers and young children.  A significant feature of the environment is the focus on natural materials and the gifts of Papatuänuku (Mother Earth).  Thoughtfully designed play spaces also contribute to high levels of enthusiasm shown by children as they interact with their learning environment.  There is an abundance of high quality equipment and resources available to children throughout each day.  Children are supported by teachers to plan and alter their environment in response to their developing interests, ideas and preferences. 

Communication:  The programme is successfully and naturally integrating the communication strand and goals of the early childhood education curriculum Te Whäriki.  Features of communication in the centre include:

  • extensive opportunities for children to discover and develop ways to express their creativity, ideas and feelings;
  • the integration of literacy and mathematical concepts in contexts that are meaningful to children’s interests, play and experimentation;
  • teacher use of a range of strategies to enhance children’s communication such as ‘baby sign language’, careful questioning, and sustained conversations with infants, toddlers and young children; and
  • opportunities for children to develop a growing awareness and appreciation of their own and other languages and cultures.

This strong emphasis on communication is contributing to a calm, respectful centre environment and to children’s development as capable and confident communicators.

Social competence:  Children experience an environment where they are encouraged to work with and alongside others.  Teachers skilfully model and promote strategies for solving conflicts, negotiation and being a good friend.  They foster an inclusive culture where all children are valued as contributing members of the group, respecting each others rights and needs.  Children are developing the skills needed to experience success in a range of familiar and new situations.

Wellbeing:  Teachers are highly attuned to individual children’s emotional and physical wellbeing.  Care routines are respectful, responsive and unobtrusively integrated into the programme.  These routines are maximised as enjoyable, unhurried learning opportunities.  A feature of the centre is the priority given to providing freshly made nutritious meals and snacks.  This approach to promoting children’s wellbeing is contributing to a positive ‘home-like’ setting where children are safe, happy and well cared for.

Professional leadership:  The centre director and co-managers have successfully established a shared vision, philosophy and expectations within a climate that fosters emergent leadership and innovation.  Since the centre opened in 2007 they have developed a focused approach to self review and strategic planning, which is providing a sound framework for ongoing improvement and centre direction.  In addition, well considered decisions about professional development have enabled a sound beginning to strengthening teacher knowledge about assessment for learning.  High quality professional leadership is positively influencing the quality of teaching and education for children and adults.

Assessment practice:  There are some examples of good quality assessment practice in the centre.  Children’s portfolios capture their strengths and developing interests through narrative writing, digital photos, movies, child’s voice and samples of work.  Teachers use questions to invite parents to contribute to their own child’s assessment and extend an open invitation to centre planning meetings.  This good assessment practice is valued by parents and enables them to play a meaningful part in the assessment of their own children’s learning and progress.

Area for improvement

Reflecting children’s learning:  Teachers are engaged in a whole-centre ‘Assessment for Learning’ contract.  The next step for the team is to continue this action research based professional reflection and learning to enhance assessment practice.  Developing common understandings about documenting and sharing the learning that comes from children’s play and exploration is likely to strengthen the consistency of assessment practice.

4 Areas of National Interest

Overview

ERO provides information about the education system as a whole through its national reports.  This information will be used as the basis for long term and systemic educational improvement.

Māori Children

As part of this review ERO evaluated the extent to which this service carries out a process to identify and respond to the aspirations and expectations of the parents and whānau of Māori children and focuses on the potential of Māori children to develop as competent and capable learners. 

Area of good performance

Respect for Māori:  The acknowledgement of the unique place of Māori as tangatawhenua and the values of kotahitanga and manaakitanga underpin all aspects of centre practice.  Teachers show genuine respect for the Māori culture through the inclusion of te reo and tikanga Māori and include meaningful opportunities for Māori children to participate as tangata whenua.  This respect for the Māori language and culture supports Māori children to reach their potential as capable and confident learners.

5 Management Assurance on Compliance Areas

Overview

Before the review, the licensee and staff of The Sandbox Preschool completed an ERO CentreManagement Assurance Statement and Self-Audit Checklist.  In these documents they have attested that they had taken all reasonable steps to meet their legislative obligations related to:

  • administration;
  • health, safety and welfare;
  • personnel management;
  • financial and property management.

During the review, ERO checked the following items because they have a potentially high impact on outcomes for children:

  • emotional safety (including behaviour management, prevention of bullying and abuse);
  • physical safety (including behaviour management, sleeping and supervision practices; accidents and medication; hygiene and routines; travel and excursion policies and procedures);
  • staff qualifications and organisation; and
  • evacuation procedures and practices for fire and earthquake.

ERO’s investigations did not identify any areas of concern.

6 Recommendation

ERO and the Centre Management agreed that:

6.1 management and teachers continue to develop team reflection, discussion and development that focuses on strengthening assessment for learning.

7 Future Action

ERO is confident that the service is being managed in the interests of the children.  Therefore ERO is likely to review the service again as part of the regular review cycle. 

Dr Graham Stoop
Chief Review Officer

GENERAL INFORMATION ABOUT REVIEWS

About ERO

ERO is an independent, external evaluation agency that undertakes reviews of schools and early childhood services throughout New Zealand.

About ERO Reviews

ERO follows a set of standard procedures to conduct reviews.  The purpose of each review is to:

  • improve quality of education for children in early childhood centres; and
  • provide information to parents, communities and the Government.

Reviews are intended to focus on outcomes for children and build on each centre’s self review.

Review Focus

ERO’s framework for reviewing and reporting is based on four review strands.

  • Quality of Education – including the quality of the programme provided for children, the quality of the learning environment and the quality of the interactions between staff and children and how these impact on outcomes for children.
  • Additional Review Priorities – other aspects of the operation of a centre, may be included in the review.  ERO will not include this strand in all reviews.
  • Areas of National Interest – information about how Government policies are working in early childhood centres.
  • Compliance with Legal Requirements – assurance that this centre has taken all reasonable steps to meet legal requirements.

Review Coverage

ERO reviews do not cover every aspect of centre performance and each ERO report may cover different issues.  The aim is to provide information on aspects that are central to positive outcomes for children and useful to this centre.

Review Recommendations

Most ERO reports include recommendations for improvement.  A recommendation on a particular issue does not necessarily mean that a centre is performing poorly in relation to that issue.  There is no direct link between the number of recommendations in this report and the overall performance of this centre.
